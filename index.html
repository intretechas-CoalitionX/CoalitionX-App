RFX Evaluator

Stage Progression Guide

Detailed guidance for each RFX stage: what to include, what to ask, success criteria and tactical advice.

Purpose

The RFI stage is about early information gathering. Your goal is to establish technical credibility and position Intretech as a serious partner before the customer has defined their requirements fully. Avoid pricing discussions — focus on engineering partnership language.

What to include in your response

  • Company overview: Intretech Group, subsidiaries, global footprint
  • Capability summary: R&D, PCBA, plastics/tooling, FATP, test systems, automation
  • Multi-site resilience: China, Malaysia, Hungary, Mexico
  • Relevant case studies by vertical (anonymised if required)
  • Footprint map: site locations, headcount, certifications
  • OEM/ODM/JDM model explanation
  • Key differentiators vs. Tier-1 EMS incumbents
  • Next steps you propose: DFM review, technical workshop, factory visit

Success criteria

Progressing to the RFQ shortlist. Getting a follow-up technical meeting or discovery call. Being invited to submit an RFQ.

Tactics

  • Lead with technical credibility, not price
  • Use engineering partnership language — "co-development", "NPI support", "EVT partner"
  • Avoid generic capability lists — tailor to their vertical and product category
  • Propose a DFM review or technical scoping session as your CTA
  • Assign a senior technical contact as the named account manager

Key discovery questions to ask the customer

  • What product category or platform is this RFI for?
  • What is the target annual volume and market region?
  • What lifecycle stage is the product at: concept, EVT, DVT?
  • Are you seeking OEM, ODM, or full co-development (JDM)?
  • What are the top three technical challenges you expect?
  • What certifications are required (CE, FCC, UKCA, ISO13485)?
  • Do you have an incumbent CM, and what is prompting this RFI?
  • What does success look like for this CM selection process?
  • Who are the key stakeholders: engineering, procurement, quality, executive?
  • What is the expected timeline to RFQ and first production?

Purpose

The RFQ stage requires a costed, commercially binding response to defined product requirements. Your goal is to be shortlisted for RFP or awarded directly. Use VA/VE framing and DFM notes to differentiate beyond price.

What to include in the RFQ response

  • BOM cost breakdown: materials, PCBA, labour, overhead
  • NRE (Non-Recurring Engineering) costs: tooling, fixtures, test jigs
  • Tooling plan: soft-to-hard tooling path, lead times
  • MOQ and annual volume pricing tiers
  • Lead times: EVT samples, DVT build, PVT ramp, FATP steady state
  • Payment terms and incoterms
  • Regional FATP options and landed-cost model
  • VA/VE suggestions: DFM improvements that reduce cost without reducing quality
  • Quality plan: IPC standards, yield targets, test coverage

Success criteria

Shortlisted for RFP or directly awarded. Customer requests a technical clarification call. You are invited to a site visit or factory tour.

Tactics

  • Frame total cost of ownership, not just unit price
  • Include VA/VE suggestions — shows engineering engagement
  • Offer a preliminary cost directionally before formal submission to reduce surprise
  • Assign an internal project owner and communicate their name to the customer
  • Include DFM notes relevant to their product category as a value-add

Key questions to ask before pricing

  • Is the BOM fully defined, or are there outstanding component decisions?
  • What are the primary and secondary PCBA layers and component count?
  • What certifications are included in scope (CE, FCC, UKCA, ISO)?
  • Is tooling in scope or is existing tooling being transferred?
  • What is the target landed cost and which incoterms apply?
  • Are there preferred component vendors or approved supplier lists?
  • What are the packaging requirements and retail channel?
  • Is there a test specification or will Intretech define test coverage?
  • What are the FATP site preferences and how are they weighted in evaluation?
  • Will this be priced as a standalone product or platform family?

Purpose

The RFP is your full strategic case for why Intretech should be selected as the manufacturing partner. This is the stage where relationship depth, technical differentiation and commercial creativity matter most. Every section should be tailored to the customer's product, team and priorities.

What to include in the RFP

  • Executive summary: why Intretech, why now, what success looks like
  • Capability proof: specific to their product category and lifecycle stage
  • Programme plan: NPI timeline, EVT/DVT/PVT gates, ramp plan
  • Quality plan: IPC standards, PFMEA, control plans, yield targets
  • Cost model: BOM, NRE, tooling, volume tiers, landed-cost comparison
  • Risk mitigation: supply chain, technical, compliance, capacity
  • Team structure: dedicated project manager, engineering lead, quality lead
  • Reference contacts: existing customers in the same vertical (if approved)
  • Proposed next steps: NDA, pilot programme, factory visit, Letter of Intent

Win strategies

  • Relationship depth: multi-threaded champion coverage across EE, ME, procurement and executive
  • Technical differentiation: engineering co-development from EVT stage, not just manufacturing
  • Commercial creativity: pilot NRE, phased volume commitment, VA/VE savings share
  • Executive sponsorship: bring in Intretech senior leadership for strategic account alignment
  • Factory visit: use a site tour to make the relationship tangible before award

Success criteria

Contract, NDA or Letter of Intent signed. Pilot programme or EVT build agreed. Customer team visits a site or requests dedicated engineering resource.

Tactical advice

  • Assign an executive sponsor from Intretech for all strategic accounts at RFP stage
  • Offer a factory visit as a standard part of the RFP response process
  • Propose a pilot programme or NRE project to establish trust before full volume commitment
  • Coach your internal champion to present Intretech's RFP internally — provide them a one-page executive summary they can forward
  • Prepare a response to the incumbent CM defence scenario: what will the customer's current CM offer to retain them?
  • Log every RFP interaction in HubSpot with contact, date, action and next step

Tactical Strategies

Six tactical playbooks for advanced selling situations. Click any strategy to expand the full guide.

When to activate executive engagement

  • The account is Category A (revenue ≥ $500M) or flagged as a VIP strategic target
  • The customer's executive is involved or the deal is stuck at a senior level
  • A competitor's executive team has already engaged the customer
  • The deal is at RFP stage with a >$5M annual value
  • A factory visit or strategic alignment meeting has been requested
  • There is an internal escalation or concern about Intretech's capacity or strategic fit

How to prepare

  • Brief the executive sponsor with a one-page account summary: account, revenue, signal, deal stage, key contacts, competitors, risks
  • Define the executive's role: figurehead sponsorship, technical credibility or commercial authority
  • Set clear objectives for the executive meeting: relationship building, deal acceleration, factory visit confirmation, or LOI discussion
  • Pre-agree talking points and avoid over-briefing — executives sell on relationship and strategic vision, not spec sheets

After the executive meeting

  • Log the interaction in HubSpot within 24 hours
  • Follow up with a tailored note from the executive that references a specific point from the conversation
  • Use the meeting to accelerate the next step: NDA, factory visit, pilot programme or LOI
  • Keep the executive engaged at key milestones — avoid going dark after the initial meeting

Why factory visits close deals

A factory visit is the single most powerful trust-building activity available in complex manufacturing sales. It makes Intretech's capabilities tangible, builds personal relationships with the customer's engineering and procurement teams, and significantly reduces the risk perception of switching from an incumbent CM.

Preparing the visit

  • Identify which capability lines are most relevant to the customer's product category
  • Prepare a visit agenda tailored to the customer's key stakeholders: engineering, procurement, quality
  • Create a customer-specific dossier: their product category, known challenges, how relevant production lines address them
  • Assign a dedicated engineering host with domain expertise in the customer's vertical
  • Prepare a clean, well-organised facility tour route — first impressions matter
  • Brief all Intretech staff who will interact with the visiting team

During and after the visit

  • Let the customer's engineers ask questions and show genuine interest — do not over-present
  • Highlight real production examples from a similar product category (with appropriate confidentiality)
  • Capture any technical concerns or questions raised during the visit as follow-up actions
  • Follow up within 48 hours with a personalised note from the Intretech host engineer
  • Propose a specific next step in the follow-up: technical scoping, DFM review, or RFX submission timeline
  • Log the visit, attendees, key discussions and follow-ups in HubSpot

Why pilots work

A pilot project or NRE engagement lowers the barrier to entry for customers evaluating Intretech against an incumbent CM. Instead of asking for a full volume commitment, you ask for a defined, bounded project where Intretech can demonstrate capabilities at low risk to the customer.

Ideal pilot scenarios

  • A new product design where no CM is yet established (greenfield NPI)
  • A product variant or SKU extension where Intretech can compete on speed and cost
  • A DFM review or feasibility study before full RFQ
  • A prototype build or EVT engagement at a fixed NRE fee
  • A regional FATP pilot for EU/US market entry while China FATP continues

Structuring the pilot proposal

  • Define clear scope, deliverables, timeline and cost
  • Propose a fixed NRE fee rather than open-ended hourly billing
  • Tie the pilot deliverables to the customer's actual evaluation criteria
  • Include a written success criteria that both parties agree to before starting
  • Assign a dedicated project manager and engineering lead from Intretech
  • Plan the pilot handover meeting as a natural bridge to the full RFQ discussion
  • Log the pilot project as a deal in HubSpot with a clear close date and next steps

Understanding the incumbent defence

When an existing CM learns they are at risk of losing an account, they will typically offer price concessions, capacity commitments or executive attention they never provided before. Your job is to help the customer understand the risk of staying with an incumbent who only improves under competitive pressure.

Strategies to counter the incumbent

  • Surface switching cost objections early — address them directly rather than hoping they won't arise
  • Demonstrate that Intretech's multi-site FATP, OEM/ODM model and engineering co-development model are structurally different from the incumbent's offer
  • Use the VA/VE and DFM framing: "We found 4 cost reduction opportunities in the first DFM review that your current CM never surfaced"
  • Coach your champion on how to frame the internal business case for switching: risk reduction, cost optimisation, supply chain resilience
  • Propose a parallel pilot or secondary product that does not immediately threaten the incumbent — establish trust before asking for a full transfer

What not to do

  • Do not criticise the incumbent directly — it shifts the conversation to loyalty rather than value
  • Do not match the incumbent's price concession without understanding the underlying economics
  • Do not rely on price alone — if your only advantage is cost, you will always be vulnerable to a counter-offer
  • Do not assume the customer champion will fight internally for you — coach them with materials they can use

Key messages to reinforce

  • "Our multi-site FATP model gives you supply chain resilience that a single-site CM cannot match"
  • "Our engineering co-development from EVT stage reduces your re-spin risk — we engage before design freeze"
  • "We have been transparent about our capabilities and cost from the first conversation — that's how we operate"

Who is your champion?

Your champion is the person inside the customer's organisation who believes in Intretech's value and is willing to advocate for you in internal meetings you cannot attend. Champions are usually in R&D, NPI, engineering or operations — rarely procurement, who are often vendor-neutral or incumbent-loyal.

Building the champion

  • Identify the champion through discovery — who is most engaged, who asks the best questions, who shares internal context?
  • Give them materials they can use internally: one-page capability summary, vertical-specific case study, VA/VE example
  • Make them look good internally — position them as the person who found the better CM, not the person pushing a switch
  • Respect their position — never ask them to do something that puts them at internal risk
  • Maintain regular contact: brief updates, relevant news, pre-meeting prep

Arming your champion

  • One-page "Why Intretech" summary with 3 bullet points for their internal audience (not a full capability deck)
  • A prepared answer to "why change CM now?" — framed around business risk and opportunity, not dissatisfaction
  • A summary of the cost and quality impact of the current situation vs. Intretech's proposed approach
  • An executive-ready slide or talking points for their internal business case presentation
  • Contact details for an Intretech reference customer in the same vertical (if approved)

Champion risk signals

  • Goes quiet after a period of active engagement
  • Stops sharing internal context or becomes more guarded
  • A competitor has re-engaged the customer at a higher level
  • Procurement has taken over the process and the champion is no longer involved

The total cost of ownership argument

Intretech is not the lowest-cost CM option in every scenario. The correct framing is total cost of ownership (TCO) — which includes unit cost, NRE, tooling, re-spin cost, quality escapes, logistics, compliance, warranty returns and time-to-market. When these are included, Intretech's model is typically more cost-efficient than low-cost alternatives for 50K–500K volume runs.

Key TCO framing points

  • Re-spin cost: a single PCB re-spin at DVT costs more than a proactive DFM review at EVT
  • Quality escapes: one field return campaign eliminates years of unit-cost savings
  • Time-to-market: a 3-month delay in launch costs more than any CM price premium
  • Tooling transfer cost: moving tooling from an incumbent CM carries hidden NRE and schedule risk
  • Compliance: getting certifications right the first time with engineering support vs. re-testing after DVT failures

How to present price

  • Never present unit cost in isolation — always contextualise with volume tier, BOM assumptions and NRE
  • Offer a directional cost estimate early in the process to avoid surprise at RFQ submission
  • Present VA/VE savings alongside cost to offset any premium perception
  • Frame regional FATP cost premium against EU/US compliance value and landed-cost reduction
  • Use the competitor matrix to show that Tier-1 EMS alternatives carry higher minimum volumes and lower responsiveness

Phrases to use

  • "The unit cost is one part of the picture — let me show you the full landed-cost model"
  • "We identified four DFM improvements that reduce BOM cost by an estimated 8–12% — that changes the economics significantly"
  • "Our Hungary FATP adds a small per-unit premium but eliminates EU import duties and 3-week logistics lead time"
